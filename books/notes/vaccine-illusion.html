2. Horse Anti-serum Mystery

Emil von Behring and Shibasaburo Kitasato utilized horse anti-serum for treating diphtheria and tetanus. The symptoms of these diseases are caused by toxins secreted under specific conditions, not the bacteria themselves. The serum, liquid component of blood, acquires anti-toxic properites within animals that has been innoculated with these toxins. This serum is them used to treat diphtheria or tetanus in humans. This serum, due to its animal host, is highly incompatible with humans. An immunologist, in 1924, discovered that if diptheria or tetanus toxins are treated with formaldehyde, the disease symptoms will not occur despite large doses. These formaldehyde-treated toxins are named toxoids and became the basis for the tetanus and diphteria vaccines. Immunologists believe that due to antibodies extensive binding capability to toxins and pathogents, some antibodies can neutralize the toxins which in turn prevents toxins from causing the symptoms of diphtheria or tetanus. In addition, antibodies to the toxins are indistinguishable from antibodies to the toxoids. There is no emperical evidence, however, that the original horse's anti-serum is dependent upon toxin-binding antibodies.

3. Natural Immunity to Tetanus - What a Surprise!

In the Journal of Experimental Medicine, edition during 1920s, is an analysis of C. tetani, the aneroibic bacteria which produces the tetanus toxin. After contact with oxygen, it becomes resilient and long-lived spores. These spores, with the catalyzying agent oxygen, germinate into bacterial cells capable of toxin porduction. By feeding guinea pigs with food containing C. tetani spores, these animals were naturally immune to the specific strain of tetanus. Following six months of this diet, the guinea pigs developed natural antibodies (agglutinins). It is also found that humans can harbor C. tetani spores in their stool without succumbing to tetanus.
